The icons — still the biggest issue on this section.
The scatter plot chart for Clinicians, the neural/head illustration for Patients, and the geometric cluster for Teams are all visually interesting in isolation, but they don't clearly communicate what they're supposed to represent at a glance. You shouldn't need to read the label to know what an icon means. Specifically the Clinicians scatter plot reads as data or analytics — not as a busy, overwhelmed provider. And the Teams geometric cluster reads as abstract art rather than communication or alignment.
The icon style itself — illustrated, colorful, somewhat complex — also feels slightly at odds with the premium, editorial tone of the rest of the page. Your hero is cinematic and confident. These cards feel like they belong to a different, friendlier brand.
Two directions worth considering:
Option 1 — Replace with simpler, more literal icons. Clean line icons that clearly represent each audience. A stethoscope or clipboard for Clinicians, a person for Patients, a group or network for Teams. Less distinctive but immediately readable.
Option 2 — Replace the icons with a number or stat. Since each card already has a compelling statistic in the body copy, you could lead with that number at large scale as the visual anchor instead of an illustration. For example:

50% — of clinician time lost to admin
1 in 2 — patients struggle to understand health information
$12,000+ — cost of poor communication per employee

That treatment would feel more premium, more data-driven, and more aligned with the kind of buyer you're targeting. It also removes the icon inconsistency problem entirely.
The card copy — good but still one step removed from your buyer.
As we discussed earlier, each card describes the audience's problem but doesn't connect it to the cost your buyer pays. This is worth fixing. The tweak is small but meaningful:

Clinicians — "Your providers spend up to 50% of their time on admin — leaving little bandwidth to learn, adopt new protocols, or deliver the care your program promises."


Patients — "Nearly 1 in 2 patients struggle to understand health information. The care you've invested in isn't landing the way it should."


Teams — "Poor internal communication costs $12,000+ per employee per year — slowing decisions and stalling the initiatives you're trying to move."

Each version ends with a consequence your buyer owns. That's the shift from empathy card to problem agitation.
"When People Pay Attention" — this label needs a rethink.
The all-caps treatment is fine but "When People Pay Attention" as a header is slightly awkward. It reads as a conditional clause waiting for a then-statement. The bullet list below it is actually functioning as that then-statement, but the visual separation makes it feel disconnected.
Consider replacing the label with something that frames it as the transformation:

When attention is earned, everything changes

Or simply lead directly into the bullets without a label at all — the transition from the problem cards to the outcome list is clear enough without a header.
The colored pill closer is the best element in this section. "Attention leads to more engagement, adherence, retention, advocacy, and referrals" with the pill treatment is distinctive, memorable, and visually ownable. Keep it exactly as is.
One structural note. The gap between the three cards and the "When People Pay Attention" block feels slightly large on this screen. It creates a visual pause that breaks the momentum. Tightening that spacing would help the section read as one cohesive argument rather than two separate blocks.
